# 7. Discussion, Ethics, and Limitations

This section contextualises the empirical results within theoretical frameworks for L2 feedback, examines pedagogical implications across learner levels, and acknowledges the ethical considerations and limitations of the current approach.

## 7.1 Interpretation of Results

The hybrid system achieved 44.20% precision on held-out data, a substantive improvement over any individual engine despite modest recall. This trade-off reflects design choices aligned with learner-centred feedback principles rather than a limitation of the underlying architecture. The precision-weighted greedy edit-selection algorithm (Section 4.3) effectively implements a specialisation strategy: each engine is treated as a domain-specific expert, and the selector routes each candidate edit to whichever engine has historically been most accurate on that ERRANT error type. LanguageTool's rule-based architecture yields perfect precision on replacement-pronoun errors and unnecessary-conjunction deletions; the LoRA-adapted flan-t5-base achieves precision 1.0 on determiner, pronoun, verb-inflection, and verb-tense replacements; the vanilla T5-small, despite its low overall accuracy, contributes narrowly on adjective-form and verb-inflection replacements (Figure 6.4). The hybrid does not outperform its constituents by averaging them; it does so by selecting which constituent to trust on each edit.

Held-out precision (44.20%) actually exceeded dev-tune precision (30.39%), indicating that the minimum-precision threshold (0.10) and the error-type priors acted as a conservative filter that generalised beyond the tuning partition. Recall remained low (6.67%), meaning the system abstains from roughly 93% of reference errors. This is a pedagogical design feature, not a defect: excessive feedback overwhelms learners, and selective, focused feedback is associated with stronger L2 uptake (Ellis, 2009; Bitchener and Storch, 2016). The mean edit-ratio of 0.9950 confirms the system's targeting: each intervention alters only a small fraction of the surface string, preserving learner voice.

The ablation condition (S5, Hybrid-NoPrior) described in Section 4.4 was designed precisely to test whether this precision gain depends on the error-type priors or merely on pooling candidate edits across three engines. While the full ablation study is reserved for future work, the engine-priors heatmap (Figure 6.4) already provides indirect evidence that the priors do substantive work: the high-precision cells are sparse and distributed differently across engines, so a uniform-prior baseline would necessarily dilute the signal that the error-type routing exploits.

## 7.2 Pedagogical Implications

Section 6.2 showed that the hybrid's highest F₀.₅ scores cluster on verb-related and spelling categories, while context-dependent categories (prepositions, punctuation) remain difficult. This empirical profile maps naturally onto CEFR-adaptive feedback scaffolding. At A-level, the system prioritises high-F₀.₅ categories (R:SPELL, R:ADJ:FORM, R:VERB:INFL, R:VERB:FORM), where accurate correction builds foundational morphosyntactic competence, and feedback is explicit and directly corrective — the learner sees the error, the correction, and a plain-language rule. At B-level, additional categories are activated (R:VERB:SVA, R:PRON) and feedback shifts toward metalinguistic annotation using grammatical terminology. At C-level, the system emphasises contextualised explanation and invites self-correction via indirect cues, consistent with the scaffolding hierarchy identified by Aljaafreh and Lantolf (1994) and discussed in Section 2.5. This progression embodies Vygotsky's (1978) zone-of-proximal-development principle: difficulty increases as competence develops, and automated feedback is configured to remain within the learner's productive reach.

## 7.3 Ethics

Deployment of automated feedback systems raises three ethical dimensions that the present work only partially addresses.

**Learner data privacy.** L2 writing is inherently personal data reflecting proficiency, first-language influence, and learning trajectory. Any production deployment must obtain informed consent specifying the use of writing samples for feedback purposes, encrypt data at rest and in transit, implement right-to-deletion protocols, and conduct data-protection impact assessments — obligations codified in the General Data Protection Regulation (European Commission, 2016).

**Bias and first-language representation.** The W&I+LOCNESS training corpus over-represents European-L1 backgrounds. Learners with non-European L1s may see systematically poorer feedback calibration, because error distributions differ across L1 groups and neural models inherit the statistical profile of their training data. A concrete instance is subject–verb agreement: the systematic error patterns of learners whose L1 uses different agreement morphology (or none) are not uniformly represented in the training distribution, so the hybrid's high per-type precision on R:VERB:SVA cannot automatically be assumed to transfer to all L1 populations. Mitigation requires transparent dataset documentation, per-L1-group performance audits, and targeted data collection from under-represented populations before institutional deployment.

**Learner autonomy.** Automated correction risks positioning the system as the arbiter of linguistic correctness rather than as a scaffold. Pedagogy informed by the output hypothesis (Swain, 1995) emphasises learners' active engagement with form; uncritical acceptance of system suggestions undermines metacognitive development. The feedback layer therefore renders suggestions dialogically, surfacing uncertainty and prompting reflection at higher CEFR levels rather than issuing prescriptive corrections.

## 7.4 Limitations

Four limitations constrain generalisation and practical deployment. First, low absolute recall (6.67%) means the system functions as a precision-focused partial-feedback tool, not a comprehensive error detector; future work should empirically optimise this trade-off through user studies. Second, the LoRA adapter at rank 16 (Section 4.2.3) is an intentionally lightweight fine-tune; larger adapters, QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023), or full fine-tuning may expand the fine-tuned model's high-precision coverage. Third, no human evaluation of the CEFR-adaptive feedback was conducted within the scope of this dissertation; the learner-judgement study described in Section 4.5.2 remains planned work, and pedagogical validity cannot be claimed until it is completed. Fourth, evaluation operated at the sentence level and ignores inter-sentential phenomena such as pronoun-chain coherence and tense consistency across clauses, which are well known to affect L2 writing quality. Extending the architecture to the document level would require context-encoding modifications and represents a natural next step. A fifth, more structural limitation concerns CEFR assignment: proficiency labels were inherited from the essay-level annotations provided in W&I+LOCNESS rather than predicted from learner output (Section 3.3). This design choice avoids the cascading-error problem documented by Vajjala and Rama (2018), but it also assumes that each learner's production is homogeneous in CEFR level across error types, whereas in practice writers may operate at different levels for different grammatical phenomena. A dynamic, per-sentence CEFR estimator would allow the feedback register to adapt at a finer granularity, at the cost of introducing the very classifier-error propagation that the current design deliberately avoids.
